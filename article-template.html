Article Title Goes Here. Make It Direct and Specific.

APR 24, 2026

A single sentence that frames the article and tells the reader why it matters to their community.

The opening paragraph sets the context. It connects the headline to the reader's world and establishes what this article will cover. Keep it concrete. Name the devices, the advisories, or the regulation that prompted this piece. The reader should know within two sentences whether this applies to them.

A second paragraph deepens the point. Reference specific CVEs, CISA advisories, or federal deadlines. Use numbers. A utility operator reading this at their desk should feel the specificity. This is not thought leadership for its own sake. It is intelligence delivered in a format they can act on.

What the advisory actually says.

Each section opens with a subheading that tells the reader what they are about to learn. The cyan left border visually separates sections and creates a scannable structure for busy readers who will not read every word.

Explain the technical details in plain language. Name the PLCs, the protocols, and the vulnerabilities. If CISA published a KEV entry, cite the CVE number and the remediation deadline. If the advisory names a specific threat actor, attribute it clearly.

Pull quotes or callouts work well for the single most important sentence in a section. Use them sparingly. One per article is usually enough.

Continue the explanation. Connect the technical finding to the operational reality of a small or mid-sized water utility. What does it mean for the plant operator who reads this? What should they do on Monday morning?

Why small utilities are the primary target.

Build the argument with evidence. Reference Muleshoe, Littleton, or other documented incidents. Link to the CISA advisory or the EPA enforcement action. Each claim should be verifiable by the reader.

Explain the structural problem. Small utilities have one IT person, zero OT security budget, and no procurement path to hire a firm that does this work. The devices were commissioned ten years ago with default credentials and have not been touched since. The network segmentation that should exist does not.

What can be done about it.

Close with actionable guidance. Name the grant programs that fund this work. Explain the timeline. Describe the first step. The reader should leave this article knowing what to do next, even if that next step is a phone call or an email.

End with a clear, direct final paragraph. No summary of what was just said. No restating the headline. Just the bottom line: what this means for the reader's community, and what Sentinel OT can do about it.
