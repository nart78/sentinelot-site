# OT Security Has a Data Problem. It's Not the One You Think.

There is a conversation happening in every corner of the OT security industry right now. It sounds roughly like this: legacy systems are running unpatched, firmware is years out of date, known vulnerabilities are sitting in production environments with no remediation timeline in sight. The problem is real. The stakes are real.

But that conversation is about one kind of data problem. There is a second one that almost nobody talks about.

Not the vulnerability data problem. The intelligence problem.

---

## The Gap No One Names

The OT security industry has spent the last decade getting better at identifying what is wrong with systems we already have access to. Penetration testers, compliance auditors, vulnerability scanners, and OT-native tools have matured significantly. If you can get inside a facility's network perimeter, you can build a fairly clear picture of what is at risk.

The gap is everything before that.

Security integrators approaching a new facility rarely have a complete picture of what they are walking into. They know a sector, a geography, a rough sense of asset class. What they typically do not have is confirmed operator identity, a verified contact who actually owns the decision, or a clear read on the exposure profile before the first conversation.

Risk underwriters are pricing policies for facilities where the underlying OT data is thin, inconsistent, or absent. They can model aggregate sector risk. What they often cannot do is underwrite a specific facility with confidence, because site-level intelligence does not exist in any standardized form.

Operators themselves rarely ask the question that matters most for their own risk posture: what does this facility look like from the outside? They know what is inside the perimeter. They rarely know what signals are visible beyond it.

Three groups. The same structural gap.

---

## Why This Is a Different Kind of Problem

Vulnerability management is an operational problem. It has tooling, processes, and governance frameworks built around it. It is hard to do well, but the roadmap is understood.

The intelligence gap is different. It is not a matter of running better tools inside the perimeter. It is a matter of what can be known about OT environments from the outside, before engagement, across a wide surface.

The information exists. Critical infrastructure environments leave signals that are visible from public-facing sources. Device identifiers. Vendor relationships. Firmware generations. Network structure. These are not secrets buried in internal systems. They are the natural byproduct of connected industrial equipment operating in environments where complete air-gapping is impractical or impossible.

The question is whether that information gets organized, verified, and delivered to the people who need it, before anything else happens.

Right now, for most of the industry, the answer is no. Not because the information is unavailable. Because nobody has built a systematic process around it.

---

## What Changes When the Gap Closes

The integrator who walks into a first conversation already holding confirmed exposure data, a verified decision-maker contact, and scope context does not have a better pitch. They have a different kind of conversation. The facility is already visible to them. The question is no longer whether there is something worth addressing. It is how to address it.

The underwriter who has site-level intelligence on a facility is not just pricing risk better. They are removing a source of structural uncertainty that currently sits under every OT policy they write. Their loss models are built on better inputs.

The operator who knows what their infrastructure looks like from outside the perimeter is not on the back foot waiting to find out what someone else already knows about them. They have a clear view of their own exposure before anyone else does.

None of this requires solving the vulnerability management problem first. It is a separate layer, and it is largely missing from the current industry stack.
