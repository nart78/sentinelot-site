# OT Integrators Are Still Finding New Customers the Hard Way

Most OT integration firms are building their pipeline the same way they did ten years ago. Industry conferences. Cold outreach to facility contacts who may or may not own the relevant budget. Vendor referrals that come in slowly and on someone else's timeline. Word of mouth from previous projects.

These methods work. But they have a ceiling, and most firms in this space have already hit it.

---

## The Ceiling on the Old Model

Conferences are expensive and slow. You meet the right people maybe twice a year, the relationship takes months to develop before anything commercial happens, and by the time it does, you are still not sure whether this facility actually has a project worth pursuing.

Cold outreach in OT integration is low-yield almost by definition. The buying decision for a SCADA upgrade or a legacy hardware replacement involves operations directors, plant managers, engineering leads, and often a capital budget approval cycle that started before you made contact. Reaching one person at a facility with a generic pitch and hoping they route you to the right conversation is not a pipeline strategy.

Vendor referrals are the best leads you will get through the old model. Pre-qualified, pre-warmed, with some built-in credibility from the vendor relationship. They also come on someone else's timeline, in someone else's volume, for someone else's reasons. You cannot build a growth model on referrals and call it a pipeline.

The deeper problem with all three is what happens when the lead comes in. You get on a call with a facility that sounds like a fit. But you do not know what they are actually running. You do not know the generation of the SCADA platform, how old the hardware is, whether the control systems have been touched in five years or fifteen. You are going into a scoping conversation without the information you need to scope it. You cannot price the project accurately, you cannot talk about realistic timelines, and you are asking the facility to trust you before you have demonstrated that you understand their environment.

That is a weak position to be in for a first conversation.

---

## What a Better Pipeline Looks Like

The integration firms that are growing faster are not doing more of the old model. They are working fewer opportunities but showing up to each one better prepared than their competition.

The difference is knowing the facility before the first call.

Confirmed asset profile: what control systems are running, what generation the hardware is, what platform the SCADA environment is built on. Scope context: enough of a picture of the environment to walk into the first conversation already talking about their situation specifically, not a generic pitch about your capabilities. A verified contact who is actually in the right part of the organization to have the conversation.

When you have that going in, the first call is a different conversation entirely. You are not prospecting. You are presenting. The facility is not evaluating whether you understand legacy OT environments in general. You have already demonstrated that you understand theirs.

## Where This Intelligence Comes From

Facilities running aging SCADA systems, legacy PLCs, and outdated control hardware leave signals that are visible from public-facing sources. Not because anyone published them, but because connected industrial equipment operating at scale generates a readable footprint over time. Platform identifiers, firmware generations, vendor relationships, installation signatures. The information exists. The question is whether it gets organized and delivered before the sales conversation happens.
