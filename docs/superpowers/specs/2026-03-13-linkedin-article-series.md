# Sentinel OT LinkedIn Article Series — Design Spec

**Date:** 2026-03-13
**Status:** Approved for planning

---

## Overview

A four-article LinkedIn series for the Sentinel OT business page. One article every three days. Each article has a single primary goal and a single target audience. Together they establish brand credibility and reach all three buyer types.

**Format:** Long-form, 800-1200 words per article. LinkedIn native articles (not posts).

**Voice:** Professional, precise. No em dashes. No methodology reveals. No mention of Shodan or specific data sources. Passive sourcing is referenced in general terms only.

---

## Series Structure

| Order | Audience | Primary Goal | Angle |
|-------|----------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | General OT industry | Thought leadership | Name the intelligence gap nobody talks about |
| 2 | Security integrators | Problem-first | Bad prospecting data costs deals before they start |
| 3 | Risk underwriters | Direct awareness | OT underwriting data hasn't kept up with the market |
| 4 | Operators | Problem-first | What does your facility look like from outside your perimeter? |

## Article 1: The Intelligence Gap

**Title:** "OT Security Has a Data Problem. It's Not the One You Think."

**Target audience:** Anyone in OT security, ICS, critical infrastructure.

**Primary goal:** Thought leadership. Establish Sentinel OT as a credible voice. Not a pitch. Sentinel OT is mentioned briefly at the close only.

**Core argument:** Everyone in OT security talks about the vulnerability data problem -- unpatched legacy systems, unmanaged CVEs, outdated firmware. That's real. But there's a quieter problem underneath it: nobody has a clear picture of what's actually out there. Operators don't know what their own infrastructure looks like from outside their perimeter. Security firms are prospecting blind. Insurers are writing policies on incomplete data. The article names this as a structural intelligence gap -- not a technology failure, but an information failure.

**Structure:**
1. Hook -- the gap nobody names (2-3 short paragraphs)
2. Three symptoms -- each buyer type experiencing the same gap differently: the integrator who gets to a facility and finds the context is wrong, the underwriter who has no site-specific OT data, the operator who has never asked what their infrastructure looks like from outside
3. What changes when the gap closes -- briefly, without describing methodology
4. One or two sentences introducing Sentinel OT as addressing this gap, with link to sentinelot.com

**What to avoid:**
- Any description of how intelligence is gathered
- Specific data sources, protocols, or vendors
- Sales language
- "Leads" framing -- this is intelligence, not lead gen

## Article 2: For Security Integrators

**Title:** "The OT Prospecting Problem Nobody Talks About"

**Target audience:** OT security firms, VARs, system integrators, ICS consultants.

**Primary goal:** Problem-first awareness. Describe the pain before offering anything.

**Core argument:** Most OT security pipelines are built on noise. IP lists. Vendor referrals. Conference contacts. The integrator gets to a qualified-looking facility, and something is off -- the exposure isn't what it seemed, the contact isn't the decision-maker, the scope is unclear. The article frames this as a structural problem with how OT intelligence reaches the sales floor. Then it describes what a structured intelligence report changes: confirmed exposure, named decision-maker contacts, scope context before the first call. The close is soft -- some firms are already working this way.

**Structure:**
1. Hook -- the familiar pre-call moment when the context falls apart
2. Why OT prospecting is structurally harder than IT (no standardized asset registries, long sales cycles, technical complexity at first contact)
3. What qualified intelligence changes -- three concrete things (confirmed exposure, named contact, scope before the call)
4. What working with structured OT intelligence looks like in practice
5. Soft close with sentinelot.com mention

**What to avoid:**
- How the intelligence is produced
- Specific protocols, vendors, or firmware mentions
- Overpromising (one article cannot close a sale)
- "Lead" as the primary framing -- prefer "intelligence" or "opportunity"

## Article 3: For Risk Underwriters

**Title:** "OT Cyber Insurance Is Growing. The Underwriting Data Hasn't Kept Up."

**Target audience:** Cyber insurance underwriters, OT risk analysts, reinsurers with ICS exposure.

**Primary goal:** Direct awareness. Name Sentinel OT explicitly as a solution to the underwriting data gap.

**Core argument:** The cyber insurance market is expanding into OT and ICS. But underwriting OT risk requires different inputs than IT risk -- legacy equipment with known CVEs that nobody is patching, facilities operating systems that haven't been updated in a decade, sectors where the exposure profile is public but the underwriting process isn't using it. The article explains the specific blind spots across oil & gas, water, power, and healthcare, then describes what structured, site-level intelligence looks like as an underwriting input. Sentinel OT is named directly as a provider.

**Structure:**
1. Hook -- the OT insurance market growing while the underlying data problem compounds
2. Why OT is structurally different to underwrite (brief -- 2-3 paragraphs)
3. The specific blind spots: what underwriters are missing about OT exposure in oil & gas, water, power, and healthcare
4. What structured site-level intelligence provides as an underwriting input
5. Direct close naming Sentinel OT with sentinelot.com

**What to avoid:**
- How the intelligence is produced
- Claiming to solve the entire underwriting problem
- Insurance regulatory language (keep it accessible)
- Specific CVE numbers or vendor names

## Article 4: For Operators

**Title:** "What Does Your Facility Look Like From Outside Your Perimeter?"

**Target audience:** Facility managers, operations directors, plant managers, infrastructure owners across oil & gas, water, power, and healthcare.

**Primary goal:** Problem-first. Ask a question they have never asked. No hard sell.

**Core argument:** Operators spend their time looking inward -- managing OT networks, patching what they can, keeping systems running. Almost nobody asks what their facility looks like from public-facing signals. The article describes, in general terms, that a significant amount of infrastructure information is visible from outside a facility's perimeter without any active probing. Not to alarm -- to inform. The argument is that knowing your own external exposure profile is a reasonable first step in understanding your risk posture. Sentinel OT is introduced as the service that provides this.

**Structure:**
1. Hook -- the question they have never been asked (2 paragraphs)
2. What is visible about a typical critical infrastructure facility from outside (general, no methodology, framed as "what an outside observer would see" not "here is what we do")
3. Why this matters -- briefly, sector by sector (oil & gas, water/wastewater, power, hospitals)
4. What knowing your own exposure profile changes
5. Soft close introducing Sentinel OT with sentinelot.com

**What to avoid:**
- Any language that sounds threatening or alarmist
- Technical specifics about protocols or data sources
- Anything that reads like a sales pitch -- this article builds trust, not urgency
- Mentioning other companies' facilities or naming real-world incidents
