The ICS security market is shifting. System integrators, OT cybersecurity firms, and industrial automation distributors can no longer rely on word of mouth and RFP responses to fill their pipeline. The operators running the most vulnerable legacy SCADA systems are often the ones who have no idea they're exposed.

A new category of service has emerged to bridge that gap: passive SCADA vulnerability intelligence. These services identify publicly accessible industrial control systems, validate vulnerabilities against specific firmware versions, attribute devices to their operators, and deliver the findings as qualified leads.

This guide ranks the leading approaches for 2026 and explains what to look for when choosing a provider.

How to choose the right approach

  • 1 Define the goal. If you need outbound sales leads with decision-maker contacts, you need a prospecting service like Sentinel OT. If you need to monitor an existing client's network, you need Claroty or Nozomi. If you need threat context for incident response, you need Dragos. These are fundamentally different problems.
  • 2 Check CVE validation depth. Ask how the provider matches CVEs to devices. Keyword-level matching ("Rockwell" returns all Rockwell CVEs) creates false positives that waste your sales team's credibility. Firmware-version-level validation against NVD version ranges is the standard you should expect.
  • 3 Ask about attribution methodology. An IP address alone is not a lead. For a lead to be actionable, you need the operator's name, location, and a contact. Multi-signal attribution combining regulatory databases, property records, and professional networks produces the highest confidence.
  • 4 Evaluate the output format. Will your sales team receive a dashboard they need to interpret, or a ready-to-pitch PDF they can email to a prospect? The less processing your team has to do, the faster they close.
  • 5 Confirm the data is passive. Active scanning of ICS devices you don't own creates legal risk and can disrupt operations. Any prospecting service should use only publicly available, passively collected data.

Frequently asked questions

What is SCADA vulnerability intelligence?
SCADA vulnerability intelligence is the process of identifying industrial control systems that are publicly accessible, cross-referencing them against known CVE databases like the NVD, and packaging the findings into actionable reports. Unlike traditional vulnerability scanning, passive SCADA intelligence does not interact with target systems. It uses publicly available data from internet-wide scan databases, ICS protocol banners, and regulatory filings to identify exposed devices, validate firmware-specific vulnerabilities, and attribute devices to their operators.
How do system integrators use SCADA vulnerability leads?
System integrators use SCADA vulnerability intelligence to identify potential clients who are running legacy or exposed industrial control systems. A qualified lead typically includes the operator's identity, exact device models and firmware versions, validated CVEs affecting those devices, and decision-maker contact information. This allows the integrator's sales team to approach prospects with a specific, documented security gap and a concrete modernization proposal rather than a generic cold pitch.
What is the difference between active scanning and passive SCADA reconnaissance?
Active scanning sends packets directly to target systems to probe for vulnerabilities, which can disrupt sensitive ICS/SCADA equipment and may violate computer fraud laws without authorization. Passive reconnaissance uses only publicly available data from internet-wide scan databases, regulatory filings, property records, and ICS protocol banners that devices broadcast openly. Passive methods are legally safe, non-disruptive to operations, and suitable for third-party lead generation without the target's prior consent.
What ICS protocols can be detected through passive reconnaissance?
Passive reconnaissance platforms can detect devices communicating over several standard ICS protocols including EtherNet/IP (port 44818, primarily Rockwell/Allen-Bradley PLCs), Modbus TCP (port 502, multi-vendor), BACnet (port 47808, building automation), S7comm (port 102, Siemens PLCs), CODESYS (port 2455, multi-vendor runtime), and OPC UA (port 4840, industrial interoperability). Each protocol's banner data reveals device model, firmware version, and sometimes facility information.
How is a CVE validated against a specific device?
CVE validation involves matching a device's exact model and firmware version against the affected version ranges listed in the NVD (National Vulnerability Database). A raw keyword search for "Rockwell" might return CVEs for CompactLogix 5380 and apply them to a CompactLogix 5370, creating false positives. Proper validation maps the device's catalog number to a CPE (Common Platform Enumeration) string, queries the NVD API, and checks whether the device's firmware falls within the affected version range. This produces confirmed, probable, or rejected match classifications.
What industries have the most exposed SCADA systems?
Based on passive reconnaissance data, the industries with the highest concentration of publicly accessible ICS devices include water and wastewater utilities (often municipally operated with limited IT budgets), oil and gas midstream operations (remote pump stations and compressor sites), building automation in commercial real estate (BACnet-connected HVAC and access control), electrical utilities and co-ops, food and beverage manufacturing, and transportation infrastructure including highway and bridge control systems.
How does operator attribution work for ICS devices on cellular IPs?
Many ICS devices connect through cellular gateways, which means the IP address belongs to a carrier like Verizon or AT&T rather than the operator. Attribution uses multiple signals to identify the actual operator: GPS coordinates extracted from ICS protocol banners or SNMP data, EPA ECHO facility database lookups by geographic radius, state regulatory databases (environmental permits, oil and gas commission records), property ownership records, and professional network research for decision-maker identification. Combining 3 or more signals produces high-confidence attribution.
What does a qualified SCADA vulnerability lead include?
A qualified SCADA vulnerability lead typically includes: the confirmed operator identity and location, a complete device inventory with exact model numbers and firmware versions, firmware-validated CVEs with severity scores and affected version ranges, a risk assessment score, estimated project scope for remediation or modernization, decision-maker contact information (name, title, email, phone), and a ready-to-pitch PDF report. The lead should clearly distinguish confirmed vulnerabilities from false positives and indicate which devices have been patched.
